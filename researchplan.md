Comprehensive Yet Realistic Evaluation Plan for Phase-Decoupled Spectral Inception in Biomedical Signal Classification

The classification of physiological time-series data, particularly electrocardiograms (ECG) and electroencephalograms (EEG), remains a central problem in machine learning for healthcare because modest improvements in class-sensitive metrics can translate directly into better screening, triage, and retrospective analysis. Over the past several years, end-to-end convolutional models have replaced a large fraction of hand-crafted feature pipelines, largely because they can learn morphology-sensitive representations directly from raw traces. Among those architectures, InceptionTime has become a strong reference point for time-series classification: it is effective, relatively efficient, and conceptually clean, combining multi-scale temporal convolutions with residual learning to capture motifs of varying duration. Yet the same design choice that gives InceptionTime its practicality also creates a limitation. Its kernels are fundamentally local in the time domain, which means the model must learn frequency-selective behavior only implicitly through stacks of convolutional filters. In biomedical signals, where diagnostic structure often depends on oscillatory content and noise frequently concentrates in particular bands, that implicit burden may be unnecessarily expensive and potentially fragile.

The proposed Phase-Decoupled Spectral Inception (PDSI) framework is intended as a lightweight modification to this baseline rather than a wholesale architectural replacement. The central idea is to introduce a single input-level spectral gate before the InceptionTime backbone, allowing the model to reweight spectral energy prior to local temporal feature extraction. The gate operates on a real FFT of the signal, separates magnitude from phase, applies a learnable mask to magnitude only, and then reconstructs the filtered signal through an inverse transform. This creates a controlled inductive bias: the model can express frequency-selective filtering explicitly, but the downstream temporal backbone remains almost unchanged. For a one-week implementation and experiment cycle, this constraint is essential. The goal is not to claim universal superiority across all biomedical time-series tasks, nor to introduce a large family of spectral architectures. The goal is to test whether a minimal, phase-decoupled gate can improve class-sensitive metrics at approximately baseline training speed under matched compute and data conditions.

This narrower framing matters because ambitious ideas become unconvincing when their evaluation scope exceeds the available time budget. A research plan that assumes full semi-supervised benchmarking, broad robustness suites, edge deployment studies, and multi-backbone expansion is misaligned with a single-week execution window, even on a strong accelerator such as an AMD MI300X. Accordingly, this revised plan retains the original scientific motivation, architectural detail, and experimental rigor, but it explicitly narrows the claim surface to what can be implemented, measured, and defended within one week. The emphasis is on a reproducible improvement-speed tradeoff, minimal causal ablations, and interpretable mechanistic evidence rather than on sweeping performance claims.

Theoretical Motivation for a Spectral Gate

The rationale for introducing a spectral gate is grounded in the structure of physiological signals themselves. ECG rhythms, seizure-related EEG discharges, and sleep-stage oscillations all contain discriminative information that is easier to describe in the frequency domain than in raw time-series form. Standard digital signal processing has exploited this fact for decades through bandpass filtering, notch filtering, and power-spectrum analysis. However, fixed DSP pipelines are limited: they require manual design choices, they do not adapt to dataset-specific or class-specific structure, and they are usually external to the learned representation pipeline. In a modern supervised classifier, the more attractive alternative is not to hand-code a filter bank for each task, but to give the model a small, interpretable mechanism that can learn where in the spectrum useful energy tends to live.

In that sense, PDSI can be viewed as a learned, task-conditioned band-selection prior. Let the input be $x \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times C \times T}$. The model computes an RFFT along the temporal dimension to obtain complex coefficients $X$. These coefficients are decomposed into magnitude and phase, $A = |X|$ and $\Phi = \angle X$. A learnable real-valued mask $M$ is applied to the magnitude, and the filtered representation is reconstructed through

$$
x_{\text{gated}} = \mathrm{iRFFT}\left((M \odot A) \cdot e^{j\Phi}\right).
$$

This formulation imposes a very specific bias. It assumes that for the first version of the method, the most useful and stable intervention is on spectral energy rather than on phase. That is a pragmatic modeling choice rather than a universal statement about signal processing. In many biomedical settings, magnitude carries the more stable class-related information, while phase can be sensitive to alignment, sensor placement, jitter, and acquisition artifacts. If the first experimental cycle is limited to one week, then magnitude-only gating is the right default because it simplifies optimization, preserves interpretability, and makes the causal role of the gate easier to test.

Why phase decoupling matters here is less about grand theoretical novelty and more about avoiding an avoidable failure mode. A naive complex-valued gate allows the model to perturb both amplitude and phase jointly, which may look more expressive on paper but is harder to optimize and harder to interpret. More importantly, it blurs the source of any gain: did performance improve because the model learned meaningful band emphasis, because it learned arbitrary phase distortions, or because the additional parameters simply enlarged the search space? By restricting the main variant to magnitude-only modulation and relegating naive complex gating to a control experiment, the study makes a sharper causal argument. If the phase-decoupled version works better or more stably at similar cost, that result is publishable even if the absolute gains are modest.

Architectural Mechanics of PDSI

The PDSI architecture should remain intentionally conservative. The baseline backbone is InceptionTime or an implementation that preserves its core design principles: an input bottleneck, parallel temporal convolutions with multiple kernel sizes, residual pathways, and a compact classification head. The new component is a single spectral gate placed at the input level before the signal enters the temporal backbone. This placement is deliberate. Inserting gates at every block would increase the architectural surface area, complicate the implementation, and introduce ambiguity about where the gain originates. A single gate offers a clean experimental story: any change in performance or stability can be attributed primarily to front-end spectral filtering rather than to a deep stack of frequency-domain modules.

The input-level gate follows four stages. First, the signal is transformed with a real FFT, which is appropriate for real-valued ECG and EEG recordings and avoids unnecessary redundancy. Second, the resulting complex spectrum is decomposed into magnitude and phase. Third, the magnitude is modulated by a learnable mask that can optionally be regularized to prevent trivial identity behavior. Fourth, the masked magnitude is recombined with the original phase and returned to the time domain with an inverse real FFT. The remainder of the network is unchanged. This design keeps the implementation burden low, the runtime overhead tractable, and the comparison against the baseline fair.

In practical terms, the gate should be parameterized as simply as possible for the first week of experiments. The mask can be initialized close to one so that optimization begins near the baseline model and only departs from it when the data support a frequency-selective adjustment. Regularization can encourage non-trivial structure without forcing an unrealistic degree of sparsity. The point is not to engineer an exotic frequency transformer; the point is to test whether a small learned perturbation of the spectrum improves the input representation enough to help a strong temporal classifier. This restraint is what makes the project feasible.

Another benefit of this design is interpretability. Because the gate acts directly on the input spectrum, the learned mask is inspectable in a straightforward way. One can average masks across samples, compare their shapes across datasets, and visualize whether the model is emphasizing low-frequency structure, attenuating high-frequency noise, or learning something degenerate. In a one-week project, this is valuable because interpretability evidence can compensate for the fact that the experimental matrix must remain small. A non-trivial, stable mask pattern across seeds is itself part of the scientific evidence.

Clinical and Signal-Processing Motivation Without Overclaiming

The biomedical justification for PDSI remains strong, but the claims must be phrased carefully. ECG and EEG signals are rich in frequency structure, and clinicians often reason about them in band-limited terms even when final diagnosis depends on waveform morphology as well. In EEG, delta, theta, alpha, beta, and gamma rhythms provide a natural vocabulary for discussing sleep stages, seizure signatures, and background activity. In ECG, baseline wander, line noise, muscle noise, and morphology-dependent arrhythmic patterns all have partially separable spectral footprints. These facts motivate a spectral gate, but they do not guarantee that a particular learnable mask will produce large gains on every dataset. The right claim is that a spectral front-end is physiologically plausible and experimentally testable, not that it automatically yields across-the-board state-of-the-art improvements.

This distinction is especially important for Sleep-EDF. Sleep staging is a compelling use case for spectral modeling because stage transitions correlate with shifts in band power, but the dataset should be treated as optional in the one-week scope. If time allows, it provides an additional validation track and a good interpretability case study; if not, the paper can still stand on CHB-MIT and PTB-XL provided the methodology and evidence are solid. The revised plan therefore preserves Sleep-EDF as a valuable expansion target rather than a mandatory pillar of the core claim.

Similarly, PTB-XL remains highly relevant because it tests whether the method is useful outside EEG and under multi-label class imbalance. However, the claim should no longer be that PDSI will beat InceptionTime by a fixed percentage margin. That level of specificity is not defensible before the experiments are run, especially under time pressure. A more realistic objective is to test whether PDSI improves Macro-F1 or balanced accuracy on at least one major track while avoiding catastrophic degradation on the others. If those gains hold across several seeds and arrive at less than 1.2x baseline epoch time, the result is meaningful.

Datasets, Protocol, and Scope Control

To make the project credible within a one-week cycle, the evaluation must be deliberately constrained. The primary datasets should be CHB-MIT and PTB-XL. CHB-MIT provides a seizure-detection-style EEG benchmark with clinically meaningful oscillatory structure and patient variability. PTB-XL provides a challenging ECG classification setting with class imbalance and broad pathology coverage. In addition, PTB-XL should be evaluated in both full-label and low-label 0.1 regimes if the existing codebase already supports that protocol, because this creates a compact way to test whether the learned spectral prior remains useful when supervision is sparse. This is not a semi-supervised claim; it is simply a reduced-label supervised setting using existing splits.

Sleep-EDF should remain explicitly optional. It is worthwhile if there is time after the two mandatory tracks and required ablations are complete, but it should not be positioned as essential to the core contribution. That single scope decision materially improves the feasibility of the project. It also improves the honesty of the written plan: a paper that thoroughly evaluates two strong tracks plus one optional extension is more credible than a paper that nominally covers three datasets but only completes the third in a rushed or inconsistent manner.

Across all compared runs, the protocol should be locked down. The same train, validation, and test split policy must be used. The same seeds must be used wherever comparisons are made. The same metric pipeline must compute Macro-F1, balanced accuracy, AUROC, and AUPRC, and the same aggregation scripts should be used to produce final tables. Secondary metrics should include epoch time, peak memory, and parameter count. These are not cosmetic additions. Because the method’s main promise is a better quality-speed tradeoff rather than raw scale, efficiency measurements are part of the central result.

Several tempting expansions should be marked out of scope for this cycle. Semi-supervised learning should be excluded. Large hyperparameter sweeps should be excluded. Broad multi-backbone benchmarking should be excluded, except possibly for one late portability check if all required experiments are already complete. Large adversarial or corruption suites should also be excluded. The one robustness evaluation that belongs in scope is a minimal, practical jitter or noise stress protocol designed to test whether the spectral gate reduces performance degradation under a plausible corruption. That is enough to support a bounded robustness claim without derailing the schedule.

Minimal Ablation Set and What It Proves

The ablation design must be minimal but sufficient for causal attribution. Four experiments are required. First, the InceptionTime baseline with no spectral gate establishes the reference point. Second, the main PDSI variant with magnitude-only gating tests the central hypothesis. Third, a naive complex spectral gating control checks whether phase decoupling actually helps compared with a less constrained alternative. Fourth, a fixed Butterworth prefilter followed by InceptionTime tests whether any observed improvement is merely due to generic denoising rather than to learned, task-specific spectral weighting.

This set is small enough to execute and large enough to tell a coherent story. If PDSI outperforms the baseline while maintaining acceptable speed, and if it also compares favorably to naive complex gating and fixed prefiltering, then the contribution is no longer just “frequency preprocessing helps.” It becomes a more precise claim: a learned, phase-decoupled, input-level spectral gate offers a better tradeoff than either doing nothing, using a fixed DSP front-end, or using a less disciplined complex-valued gate. That is a defensible and focused main-track claim.

An alternate-backbone portability check can remain optional, but it should be treated as a bonus rather than as required evidence. If the main experiments already show a stable gain on CHB-MIT or PTB-XL and the masks are interpretable, a brief portability experiment strengthens the narrative. If time is tight, it should be dropped without hesitation.

Success Criteria, Target Outcomes, and Stop Criteria

A realistic project needs success criteria that are meaningful but not inflated. The core efficiency target is that PDSI should run at no more than 1.2x the epoch time of the baseline under matched settings. That threshold preserves the argument that the method is lightweight. The core predictive target is not universal superiority across every benchmark. Instead, the method should improve Macro-F1 or balanced accuracy on at least one major dataset track, while avoiding severe regressions on the others. In addition, the gains should not appear only in a single lucky seed; they should be directionally consistent across multiple seeds. Finally, the learned masks should be non-trivial and interpretable, rather than collapsing to an identity-like transformation.

These criteria imply a corresponding set of stop criteria. If, after initial CHB-MIT and PTB-XL runs, the mask remains effectively identity across seeds and epochs, then the gate is not doing useful work and the project should pivot. If runtime exceeds 1.5x the baseline at matched batch settings, then the method is too expensive relative to its promise and should either be simplified or reframed. If no meaningful validation improvement appears on any target metric, then the paper should avoid overselling the idea and instead either simplify the gate or reposition the result as a diagnostic negative finding. A coarse band-limited gate with stronger regularization is a reasonable fallback if the fully learnable mask proves unstable.

Framing the stop criteria explicitly is not a sign of weakness. It is part of good experimental design. One-week projects fail when they continue investing in a hypothesis after the empirical signal has disappeared. By contrast, a paper that states in advance how it would recognize collapse, runtime violation, or lack of signal reads as more rigorous.

Runtime, Hardware, and Feasibility on a Single MI300X

The AMD MI300X makes this plan plausible, but the hardware should not be used as an excuse to expand the scope. The model remains lightweight partly because FFT operations on the sequence lengths typical of ECG and EEG are modest relative to the downstream convolutional workload, especially when implemented with optimized libraries and mixed precision where stable. The extra overhead consists primarily of a forward spectral transform, an element-wise mask application, and an inverse transform. For the input-level gate, that overhead should remain materially smaller than the cost of a deep backbone. This is why the project can reasonably target near-baseline training speed.

Still, the plan should avoid claiming measured timings before those timings exist. The responsible wording is that the gate is expected to add only moderate overhead and that this expectation will be tested directly through matched epoch-time measurements. If the observed overhead stays around the 1.1x to 1.2x range, the efficiency argument is supported. If not, the plan already defines the threshold at which the project should pivot.

One-Week Execution Strategy

The execution schedule should be concrete. Day 1 is for implementation and sanity checking: integrate the PDSG module into the baseline classifier path, add configuration flags for gate mode and regularization, verify shape correctness and numerical stability, and measure single-epoch overhead. Day 2 is for CHB-MIT baseline versus PDSI under matched conditions, with seed-level metrics, timing, and early mask inspection. Day 3 repeats that pattern for PTB-XL, including the low-label 0.1 setting if the existing scripts support it cleanly. Day 4 is the decisive ablation day: run the naive complex gate and fixed-prefilter controls, evaluate the stop criteria, and lock the final variant and claim scope. Day 5 is reserved for one minimal robustness protocol, such as jitter or high-frequency noise corruption. Day 6 aggregates results and produces the small set of necessary figures: quality-speed tradeoff, per-dataset performance comparison, and spectral mask visualization. Day 7 is for writing, limitation framing, and reviewer-risk reduction.

This schedule is intentionally front-loaded. The purpose is to detect failure modes early, before time is spent polishing an unsupported narrative. If the gate collapses or the runtime is too high by Day 4, the project can still produce a credible outcome by simplifying the method or by writing a compact negative-result section that explains why the learned mask did not provide sufficient signal under the tested constraints.

Claim Language for a Defensible Paper

The claims in the final paper should be conservative by design. Good wording includes: PDSI introduces a lightweight phase-decoupled spectral gate that improves class-sensitive metrics while preserving near-baseline training speed; under matched data and compute settings, PDSI provides consistent gains on selected biomedical classification tracks; and the learned spectral masks provide interpretable evidence of frequency-selective behavior. These are strong claims, but they remain conditional, measured, and falsifiable.

What should be avoided is equally important. The paper should not promise universal state-of-the-art performance. It should not state fixed percentage improvements before all experiments are complete. It should not claim semi-supervised superiority, broad domain generalization, or deployment readiness unless dedicated experiments are actually run. In short, the revised plan is built to survive reviewer scrutiny by making only the claims that the one-week evidence base can sustain.

Interpretability and Reviewer-Facing Value

One of the most useful secondary outcomes of this project is that even a modestly positive result can be explained mechanistically. If the learned mask on CHB-MIT consistently emphasizes seizure-relevant bands or suppresses high-frequency nuisance components, that is informative even if the metric gain is moderate. If on PTB-XL the gate shows non-trivial structure that differs from a fixed prefilter and improves Macro-F1 under imbalance, that strengthens the argument that the module is learning task-specific spectral structure rather than simply denoising. These visualizations are not decorative. They help answer the reviewer question, “Why should this work?” with direct evidence.

Equally, interpretability can reveal failure. If the masks remain flat, unstable, or inconsistent across seeds, then the project learns something important about the limits of a single input-level spectral gate. That finding may still be valuable, but it should lead to a narrower paper or a pivot rather than to inflated claims.

Conclusion

Phase-Decoupled Spectral Inception remains a worthwhile research direction, but the paper plan must match the time budget. The most credible version of this project is not an attempt to prove universal dominance across every biomedical benchmark in one week. It is a tightly scoped investigation of whether a single, interpretable, magnitude-only spectral gate can improve a strong InceptionTime-style baseline on CHB-MIT and PTB-XL, optionally extending to Sleep-EDF if time remains, while preserving near-baseline efficiency and supporting its behavior with minimal but well-chosen ablations.

Under that framing, the contribution is still meaningful. It offers a clear architectural idea, a plausible physiological motivation, a compact causal evaluation design, explicit stop criteria, and a realistic path to a reviewer-defensible result. If the experiments succeed, the outcome is a concise but publishable story about an effective spectral inductive bias for biomedical time-series classification. If they fail, the revised plan still leaves room for a principled pivot rather than an overextended narrative. That is the right posture for a one-week NeurIPS push.
